Persistence vs. Nonpersistence

Home/ Persistence

1. Babies & Lactase

At birth, the majority of infants make a lot of lactase since their primary source of energy, breast milk, contains a lot of lactose. No lactase could mean death, as without it, infants would not be able to properly digest the breast milk.

2. The Default vs the Mutation

Despite lactase being vital for survival in the first weeks to months of life for mammals, lactase expression heavily decreases throughout childhood and adulthood. This decline is the "default." However, some humans can keep producing lactase into adulthood, which is an ability that has appeared only in the last 10,000 years (2).

Lactase persistence (the ability to keep producing lactase after weaning) is caused by a mutation to a single nucleotide (a SNP) in an intron of the MCM6 gene. The MCM6 gene sits just upstream of LCT. A single difference in that MCM6 intron — a structural change — causes a cascade of molecular events that can result in lactase persistence.

These events include transcription factors binding more readily to the MCM6 enhancer region, which helps bend the DNA in a way that ultimately increases transcription of the just-downstream LCT gene. More transcription of LCT leads to more mRNA, more translation, more of the enzyme, and therefore, a better ability to digest lactose.
