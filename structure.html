1. Overview on lactase synthesis

Lactase is produced in the enterocytes lining the small intestine. The enzyme is encoded by the LCT gene on chromosome 2. As it is a protein, lactase is made through the processes of transcription and translation, but it also undergoes several additional steps in order to become functional. To become mature lactase, the protein goes through the forms: pre-pro-lactase, pro-lactase, and finally, mature lactase. During its progression, the protein goes from the cytoplasm to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER) and then to the Golgi apparatus (2). Ultimately, it is delivered to the apical surface of the enterocyte.

2. Pre-pro-lactase, pro-lactase, and mature lactase

Lactase's three forms each aid in the process of synthesizing lactase for its ultimate function of breaking down lactose.

  1. Pre-pro-lactase: Lactase is first synthesized as a precursor protein called pre-pro-lactase. The "pre" part of the name refers to the signal peptide. The structure of this signal peptide, specifically with its hydrophobic core, is essential for its function of attracting the signal recognition particle (SRP) (3). The SRP brings the protein and its ribosome to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER), which is essential, as without the SRP recognizing the signal peptide and moving the protein to the RER, the protein would eventually remain in the cell, instead of being transported to the cell membrane where it could meet lactose.
  2. Pro-lactase: In the RER, the signal peptide is removed, leading to pro-lactase. Pro-lactase undergoes glycosylation, in which sugar chains are added to the amino acid sequence (2). This step is important for ensuring proper protein folding and stability, particularly in the harsh environment of the small intestine, where the protein will end up (4). Once in the Golgi, the "pro" part of the sequence helps to prevent degradation and ensures that the folding occurs properly (6).
  3. Mature lactase: Once the pro-region is cleaved from the precursor protein in the Golgi apparatus, lactase reaches its functional form. This mature-lactase enzyme contains two active sites, one of which is specifically shaped to bind to lactose and hydrolyze it into glucose and galactose. Mature lactase is then transported to the brush border membrane, where it can meet the lactose as it passes through the small intestine.

3. Why lactase's location matters

Lactase sits on the apical surface of the enterocyte. The majority of the protein points toward the lumen of the intestine, while the rest is embedded within the membrane to anchor the protein in place.

Two structural features make lactase's location great for its function:

  • On lactose's path: Since lactase is attached to the enterocyte's membrane, it is placed directly where lactose flows in the small intestine. This placement allows lactase to bind and more readily hydrolyze the lactose. Additionally, lactase is located close to the transport proteins (such as SGLT1) that bring glucose and galactose into the enterocyte, and subsequently the bloodstream, after lactose is broken down, which increases efficiency.
  • Huge surface area. The microvilli on the apical surface of the enterocyte increase the surface area, as their many small extensions push the membrane outward, allowing more spaces for lactase to bind to lactose, ultimately increasing the efficiency of lactose digestion (1) (8).
