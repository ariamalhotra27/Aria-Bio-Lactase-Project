Lactose's Journey

Home/ Lactose's Journey

1. From mouth to stomach

Upon drinking milk, or another food containing lactose, the lactose travels from the mouth to the esophagus to the stomach essentially unchanged.

2. Into the small intestine

The lactose passes from the stomach into the first part of the small intestine, the duodenum, and then the middle section, the jejunum. It is at the jejunum where lactose meets lactase: the enzyme that can split it (2).

3. Lactose meets lactase

At the brush border of the jejunum, lactose collides with lactase, which is anchored on the surface of an enterocyte. Lactase performs hydrolysis, which entails using a water molecule to split the bond that holds the disaccharide together. Once hydrolysis has been performed, lactose is now broken down into a molecule of glucose and galactose (7).

Both products are monosaccharides, which is crucial since they are now small enough to be absorbed into the enterocyte, and subsequently, into the bloodstream. While this movement of glucose does still involve help (the help of SGLT1 and GLUT2 transporters), the process would not have been possible without the lactase to break it down.
